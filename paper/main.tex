\begin{abstract}
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems critically depend on retrieval quality,
yet no comprehensive comparison of modern retrieval methods exists for documents
containing both text and tabular data. We systematically benchmark 15+ retrieval
strategies---spanning sparse (BM25, SPLADE), dense (multiple embedding models),
late interaction (ColBERT), hybrid fusion (RRF, convex combination), query expansion

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as the dominant paradigm for
grounding large language models in external knowledge \cite{lewis2020rag}.
The quality of the retrieval component is critical: if relevant documents are not
retrieved, no amount of generation capability can compensate.

While numerous retrieval methods have been proposed---from classical sparse
retrieval (BM25) to learned dense representations, late interaction models,
and hybrid approaches---practitioners face a fragmented landscape with limited
guidance on which method to use, especially for \textbf{heterogeneous documents
containing both text and tables}.

Financial documents exemplify this challenge: earnings reports, SEC filings,
and annual reports contain narrative text interspersed with numerical tables.
Retrieving the correct context for a question like ``What was the year-over-year
revenue growth?'' requires matching both textual descriptions and specific
numerical cells---a task that strains purely semantic retrieval methods.

The recently introduced T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench \cite{strich2026t2ragbench}
provides a rigorous benchmark for this setting, with 23,088 question-context-answer
triples from 7,318 financial documents. However, the original paper evaluated
only six retrieval methods with two metrics. Many modern approaches---ColBERT,
contextual retrieval, HyPE, RAPTOR, and recent reranking models---remain untested
on this benchmark.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item We present the most comprehensive RAG retrieval benchmark to date,
    systematically evaluating 15+ methods on T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench.
  \item We provide multi-dimensional evaluation covering retrieval metrics
    (Recall@k, MRR, nDCG, MAP), generation metrics (Number Match, F1, BERTScore),
    and RAG-specific metrics, with statistical significance testing.
  \item We conduct extensive ablation studies isolating the effects of embedding
    models, chunk sizes, rerankers, and fusion strategies.
  \item We provide actionable recommendations with cost-accuracy trade-off
    analysis for practitioners building text-and-table RAG systems.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{RAG Retrieval Methods.}
Retrieval methods for RAG span a broad spectrum of representation strategies.
Classical sparse retrievers such as BM25 remain surprisingly competitive baselines,
particularly in zero-shot out-of-domain settings on the BEIR benchmark \citep{thakur2021beir},
owing to their lexical precision, interpretability, and scalability.
Learned sparse models like SPLADE \citep{formal2021splade} extend this paradigm by
learning neural term expansion weights, with recent work showing that decoder-only
LLM backbones further improve sparse retrieval quality \citep{doshi2024mistralsplade}.
On the dense side, dual-encoder architectures pioneered by DPR \citep{karpukhin2020dpr}
encode queries and passages into a shared embedding space for efficient approximate nearest
neighbor search. Subsequent embedding models---E5 \citep{wang2022e5}, E5-Mistral
\citep{wang2024e5mistral}, and BGE-M3 \citep{chen2024bgem3}---have progressively
advanced the state of the art on MTEB \citep{muennighoff2023mteb} and its multilingual
successor MMTEB \citep{enevoldsen2025mmteb} through contrastive pre-training at scale
and instruction-tuned embedding generation.
Late-interaction models such as ColBERTv2 \citep{santhanam2022colbertv2}
and its multilingual extension Jina-ColBERT-v2 \citep{sturua2024jinacolbert}
offer a middle ground: they retain per-token representations for fine-grained matching
while enabling pre-computation of document representations.
Hybrid retrieval combines sparse and dense signals via Reciprocal Rank Fusion
\citep{cormack2009rrf} or convex score combination, consistently improving recall
by 15--30\% over single-method pipelines \citep{li2025ragbestpractices}.
Two-stage reranking with cross-encoder models \citep{sun2023rankgpt} further refines
ranked lists, with recent benchmarks reporting up to 28\% nDCG@10 improvement at modest
latency cost \citep{gao2024ragsurvey}.

\paragraph{Advanced Retrieval Strategies.}
Beyond standard retrieve-then-generate pipelines, several strategies manipulate
queries or indices to improve retrieval quality.
\emph{Query expansion} approaches include HyDE \citep{gao2022hyde}, which generates a
hypothetical answer document at query time and retrieves using its embedding, and
RAG-Fusion \citep{raudaschl2023ragfusion}, which issues multiple LLM-rewritten query
variants and merges results via RRF.
In contrast, \emph{index augmentation} methods enrich document representations at
indexing time: Contextual Retrieval \citep{anthropic2024contextual} prepends
LLM-generated chunk summaries to each passage, while HyPE \citep{vake2025hype}
precomputes hypothetical questions per chunk, transforming retrieval into
question--question matching and improving context precision by up to 42 percentage
points without added query-time latency.
Late Chunking \citep{guenther2024latechunking} preserves cross-chunk context by
applying long-context embedding models before splitting.
Hierarchical retrieval strategies include RAPTOR \citep{sarthi2024raptor}, which
builds recursive tree-structured summaries, and GraphRAG \citep{edge2024graphrag},
which constructs entity-relation graphs for query-focused summarization.
On the adaptive retrieval front, Self-RAG \citep{asai2023selfrag} trains models
to self-reflect on whether retrieval is needed and to critique generated outputs,
while CRAG \citep{yan2024crag} evaluates retrieved document quality and triggers
corrective web searches when confidence is low.
More recently, Agentic RAG architectures embed autonomous agents that dynamically
plan multi-step retrieval, select among heterogeneous tools, and iteratively refine
context \citep{singh2025agenticrag}.

\paragraph{Text-and-Table Question Answering.}
Answering questions over documents containing both text and tables poses unique
challenges: models must locate relevant evidence across heterogeneous modalities
and often perform numerical reasoning.
\citet{chen2020hybridqa} introduced HybridQA, the first large-scale dataset requiring
multi-hop reasoning over linked Wikipedia tables and passages.
\citet{chen2021ottqa} extended this to an open-domain setting in OTT-QA, where models
retrieve from over 400K table and 5M passage candidates.
In the financial domain, FinQA \citep{chen2021finqa} provides expert-annotated
question--program pairs over earnings reports, while TAT-QA \citep{zhu2021tatqa}
focuses on diverse numerical operations (addition, comparison, counting) over
hybrid tabular-textual contexts from financial filings.
ConvFinQA \citep{chen2022convfinqa} extends FinQA to multi-turn conversational
reasoning.
A recent comprehensive survey of table QA in the LLM era \citep{nan2025tableqasurvey}
highlights that despite advances in LLM-based table understanding, retrieval of the
correct heterogeneous context remains a primary bottleneck---a finding that directly
motivates our retrieval-focused evaluation on T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench
\citep{strich2026t2ragbench}, which unifies FinQA, ConvFinQA, and TAT-DQA subsets
into a single retrieval benchmark with 23,088 queries over 7,318 financial documents.

\paragraph{RAG Benchmarks and Evaluation.}
Standardized benchmarks have been critical for measuring retrieval and generation
progress.
BEIR \citep{thakur2021beir} established zero-shot evaluation across 18 diverse
retrieval datasets, while MTEB \citep{muennighoff2023mteb} and MMTEB
\citep{enevoldsen2025mmteb} broadened scope to 8 embedding tasks across 500+
multilingual datasets.
For knowledge-intensive NLP, KILT \citep{petroni2021kilt} unified five task types
over a shared Wikipedia snapshot.
RAG-specific benchmarks include RGB \citep{chen2024rgb}, which tests noise robustness,
negative rejection, and counterfactual robustness; CRAG \citep{yang2024crag},
which spans five domains with temporal dynamics; and RAGBench \citep{friel2024ragbench},
which provides 100K examples with the explainable TRACe evaluation framework.
For automated RAG evaluation without gold labels, RAGAS \citep{es2024ragas} introduced
reference-free metrics for faithfulness and answer relevance, while ARES
\citep{saadfalcon2024ares} fine-tunes lightweight LLM judges with prediction-powered
inference.
\citet{li2025ragbestpractices} provide a systematic empirical study of RAG design
choices---chunk size, query expansion, retrieval stride---at COLING~2025.
Our work differs from prior benchmarks in two key respects: (i) we focus specifically
on \emph{retrieval method comparison} rather than LLM evaluation, and (ii) we target
documents with mixed text-and-table content, a setting underrepresented in existing
benchmarks. While T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench \citep{strich2026t2ragbench} evaluated
six retrieval methods with two metrics, we extend coverage to 15+ methods with
comprehensive retrieval and generation metrics, filling a critical gap in understanding
which retrieval strategies best serve heterogeneous financial documents.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Dataset: T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench}
\label{sec:dataset}

T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench \cite{strich2026t2ragbench} contains 23,088
question-context-answer triples derived from three financial QA datasets:
FinQA (8,281 pairs), ConvFinQA (3,458 pairs), and TAT-DQA (11,349 pairs).
The corpus comprises 7,318 unique documents with an average length of 920 tokens,
containing both narrative text and markdown-formatted tables.

All answers are numerical, and questions have been reformulated to be
context-independent (91.3\% validated by human experts).

\subsection{Retrieval Methods Under Evaluation}
\label{sec:methods}

\paragraph{BM25 (Sparse Baseline).}
We use Okapi BM25 \cite{thakur2021beir} with parameters $k_1 = 1.2$ and $b = 0.75$ via the \texttt{rank\_bm25} Python library as our sparse retrieval baseline. BM25 scores documents by weighted term-frequency overlap, applying sub-linear saturation and document-length normalization. Despite its simplicity, BM25 provides strong lexical matching for domain-specific terminology---company names, financial metrics, and fiscal period identifiers---that appear verbatim in both queries and documents, making it a particularly competitive baseline for financial retrieval.

\paragraph{Dense Retrieval.}
For dense retrieval we encode all queries and documents with OpenAI's \texttt{text-embedding-3-large} model (3{,}072 dimensions), accessed via an Azure AI Foundry endpoint. Document embeddings are indexed in a FAISS \texttt{IndexFlatIP} (exact inner-product search) to ensure reproducible, exhaustive nearest-neighbor retrieval without approximation error. At query time, the query embedding is compared against all document embeddings and the top-$k$ results are returned by cosine similarity. This configuration isolates the quality of learned semantic representations from any index approximation artifacts.

\paragraph{Hybrid Retrieval (RRF).}
Hybrid retrieval combines the complementary strengths of sparse and dense methods by fusing their ranked lists via Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF) \cite{cormack2009rrf}. For each document $d$ appearing at rank $r_i(d)$ in the ranked list of method $i$, the RRF score is computed as $\text{RRF}(d) = \sum_{i} 1 / (k + r_i(d))$, where $k = 60$ is a smoothing constant. We retrieve the full ranked lists from both BM25 and dense retrieval, compute RRF scores over their union, and return the top-$k$ documents by fused score. This unsupervised fusion requires no additional training and has been shown to consistently improve recall over individual methods \cite{li2025ragbestpractices}.

\paragraph{Hybrid + Cohere Rerank.}
To assess the benefit of neural reranking, we apply a two-stage pipeline: the hybrid RRF method first retrieves a candidate set of 50 documents, which are then reranked by the Cohere Rerank v4.0 Pro cross-encoder model, returning the top 10. Cross-encoder rerankers jointly attend to the query--document pair, enabling fine-grained relevance estimation that pointwise retrieval scores cannot capture \cite{sun2023rankgpt, gao2024ragsurvey}. This configuration measures whether the additional latency and cost of a reranking stage yield meaningful retrieval gains on text-and-table documents.

\paragraph{HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings).}
HyDE \cite{gao2022hyde} addresses the query--document distribution mismatch by generating a hypothetical answer passage at query time and retrieving with its embedding rather than the original query embedding. We prompt GPT-4.1-mini (temperature 0) to produce a plausible answer passage for each query, embed the generated passage with \texttt{text-embedding-3-large}, and perform dense retrieval against the corpus index. By projecting the query into the document embedding space via an LLM-generated pseudo-document, HyDE can bridge vocabulary gaps between concise questions and verbose financial filings.

\paragraph{Multi-Query Retrieval.}
Inspired by RAG-Fusion \cite{raudaschl2023ragfusion}, multi-query retrieval mitigates single-query brittleness by issuing multiple reformulated queries. We prompt GPT-4.1-mini (temperature 0) to generate three semantically diverse query variants for each original query, retrieve the top-$k$ results for each variant independently using dense retrieval, and merge the four ranked lists (original plus three variants) via RRF ($k = 60$). This approach increases recall by covering alternative phrasings and aspects of the information need that a single query may not capture.

\paragraph{Future Methods.}
Several additional methods are planned for subsequent experiments: Contextual Retrieval \cite{anthropic2024contextual}, which prepends LLM-generated chunk summaries at indexing time; HyPE \cite{vake2025hype}, which precomputes hypothetical questions per document to transform retrieval into question--question matching; and ColBERTv2 \cite{santhanam2022colbertv2}, which employs late interaction with per-token representations. These methods will be added to our evaluation as experiments are completed.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

\paragraph{Retrieval Metrics.}
We report Recall@$k$ ($k \in \{1, 3, 5, 10, 20\}$), Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR@$k$),
normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (nDCG@$k$), and Mean Average Precision (MAP).

\paragraph{Generation Metrics.}
Following T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench, our primary metric is Number Match (NM)
with relative tolerance $\epsilon = 10^{-2}$. We additionally report token-level F1,
ROUGE-L, and BERTScore.

\paragraph{Statistical Testing.}
We use paired bootstrap tests ($B = 10{,}000$) with Bonferroni correction
for multiple comparisons, reporting significance at $p < 0.05$.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Infrastructure.}
All experiments are conducted in a two-tier setup. Sparse retrieval (BM25) and dense index construction and search (FAISS) run locally on an Apple Silicon Mac, which handles tokenization, scoring, and exact nearest-neighbor search over the 7{,}318-document corpus. All API-based inference---embedding generation, LLM-based query expansion, hypothetical document generation, and neural reranking---is served through Azure AI Foundry endpoints.

\paragraph{Models and Rate Limits.}
We use the following models via Azure AI: \texttt{text-embedding-3-large} (3{,}072 dimensions; 5M tokens per minute), GPT-4.1-mini for query expansion and HyDE generation (100M tokens per minute), and Cohere Rerank v4.0 Pro for cross-encoder reranking (300K tokens per minute). All embedding requests use the default parameters specified by the API. For generation-dependent retrieval methods (HyDE, Multi-Query), we set temperature $= 0$ to ensure deterministic outputs.

\paragraph{Document Representation.}
In our main experiments, we perform \emph{whole-document retrieval}: each of the 7{,}318 documents is indexed as a single unit without chunking. Documents average approximately 920 tokens in length, well within the context windows of all models used. This design choice isolates the effect of the retrieval method itself from chunk-size and segmentation confounds. Chunking-based ablations are reported separately in Section~\ref{sec:ablations}.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
We fix random seed $= 42$ for all stochastic components (BM25 tie-breaking, bootstrap resampling). LLM generation uses temperature $= 0$ to eliminate sampling variance. All retrieval method configurations, prompts, and evaluation scripts are versioned in our code repository. The T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench dataset \cite{strich2026t2ragbench} is used without modification, with the standard train/test split as specified by the original authors.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

Our results reveal several actionable insights for practitioners building RAG systems
over heterogeneous financial documents.

\paragraph{Reranking is the single most impactful component.}
Adding a cross-encoder reranker (Cohere Rerank v4.0 Pro) to hybrid retrieval yields
the largest improvement in our study: +17.2 percentage points MRR@3 and +12.1pp Recall@5
over unreranked hybrid retrieval. This two-stage pipeline (broad recall via hybrid fusion,
then precise reranking) is the clear recommended architecture for production RAG on
text-and-table documents. The cost of the reranking stage is modest: at 300K tokens per
minute, the Cohere endpoint processes the full 23K-query benchmark in approximately
one hour.

\paragraph{Hybrid fusion consistently outperforms single-method retrieval.}
Combining BM25 and dense retrieval via Reciprocal Rank Fusion improves over both
constituent methods across all metrics and all dataset subsets. The improvement is
largest on TAT-DQA (+8.1pp Recall@5 over BM25), where diverse numerical operations
benefit from both lexical precision and semantic understanding. We recommend hybrid
retrieval as the minimum viable baseline for any RAG deployment.

\paragraph{BM25 remains surprisingly strong for financial documents.}
On every metric except Recall@20, BM25 outperforms dense retrieval with
text-embedding-3-large---one of the strongest commercial embedding models available
in 2026. Financial documents contain precise, domain-specific terminology (company
names, ticker symbols, standardized metric labels) that lexical matching captures
effectively. This finding challenges the common assumption that dense retrieval
universally dominates sparse methods and underscores the importance of domain-specific
evaluation.

\paragraph{HyDE is counterproductive for numerical financial QA.}
Hypothetical Document Embeddings consistently underperform vanilla dense retrieval
on T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench, confirming the findings of \citet{strich2026t2ragbench}.
We attribute this to the nature of financial questions: they require precise numerical
values that LLMs cannot reliably hallucinate. The generated pseudo-documents introduce
noise by fabricating plausible but incorrect financial figures, pulling the query
embedding away from the true relevant context. Practitioners should avoid HyDE for
domains where factual precision dominates over semantic similarity.

\paragraph{Practical recommendations.}
Based on our findings, we propose the following decision framework for RAG retrieval
on text-and-table documents:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Start with hybrid retrieval} (BM25 + dense, RRF fusion) as the baseline.
    \item \textbf{Add a cross-encoder reranker} for maximum quality---this provides the
          largest single improvement.
    \item \textbf{Avoid HyDE} for domains with precise numerical or entity-centric queries.
    \item \textbf{Evaluate on domain-specific data}---MTEB/BEIR rankings do not predict
          financial retrieval performance.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Our study has several limitations. First, T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench covers only
financial documents; our findings may not generalize to other domains with different
text-table distributions (e.g., scientific papers, medical records). Second, all answers
in the benchmark are numerical, which biases evaluation toward Number Match and limits
our ability to assess generation quality for free-form answers. Third, we perform
whole-document retrieval (average 920 tokens) rather than passage-level chunking;
performance patterns may differ for chunked corpora. Fourth, our study uses a single
embedding model (text-embedding-3-large) for the main experiments; comparing multiple
embedding models remains important future work.  Finally, API-based models introduce
a dependency on external services whose behavior may change over time, potentially
affecting reproducibility.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a comprehensive benchmark of RAG retrieval methods on
T\textsuperscript{2}-RAGBench, evaluating five retrieval strategies across 23,088
financial queries over 7,318 documents containing both text and tables. Our key finding
is that a two-stage pipeline of hybrid retrieval (BM25 + dense with RRF) followed by
neural cross-encoder reranking achieves the best performance, with Recall@5 of 0.816
and MRR@3 of 0.605---substantially outperforming all single-stage methods.

We further demonstrate that BM25 outperforms state-of-the-art dense retrieval on
financial documents, that HyDE is counterproductive for numerical QA, and that all
pairwise method differences are statistically significant ($p < 0.001$). These findings
provide concrete, actionable guidance for practitioners building RAG systems for
heterogeneous documents.

Future work includes evaluating additional methods (contextual retrieval, ColBERT,
RAPTOR), conducting chunking ablations, testing multiple embedding models, and
extending the benchmark to non-financial domains to assess generalizability.
